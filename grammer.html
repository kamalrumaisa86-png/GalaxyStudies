📘 English Grammar (Detailed Notes)

Nouns

A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. It is the "naming word" of a sentence.

Formula: Noun = Name of (Person / Place / Thing / Idea)

Main Categories of Nouns • Common: General (e.g., city, dog). • Proper: Specific and capitalized (e.g., Paris, Rover). • Concrete vs. Abstract: • Concrete: Physical things you can touch (e.g., table, pizza). • Abstract: Ideas or feelings (e.g., bravery, love).

Countable vs. Uncountable • Countable: Things you can count (e.g., one book, two books). • Uncountable: Masses or concepts that cannot be counted (e.g., water, advice, luggage).

Collective Nouns Words that represent a group as a single unit (e.g., team, family, herd).

Singular vs. Plural • Singular: One item (e.g., cat). • Plural: More than one item (e.g., cats, children).

Quick Tip If you can put "the", "a", or "an" in front of the word, it is likely a noun!

Meaning: Any word that gives a name to something is a noun.

Examples: teacher, Dhaka, book, happiness

Verbs

Verbs express actions, occurrences, or states of being. Every complete sentence must have a verb.

Formula: Sentence = Subject + Verb

Main Verb Categories • Action Verbs: Physical or mental activity. (e.g., Run, Think, Eat). • Linking Verbs: Connect the subject to a description. (e.g., Is, Seems, Feels). • Helping (Auxiliary) Verbs: Combine with a main verb to show tense or mood. (e.g., Have, Will, Can).

Regular vs. Irregular Verbs • Regular: Add -ed for past tense (e.g., Walk -> Walked). • Irregular: Change form completely (e.g., Go -> Went, Buy -> Bought).

Transitive vs. Intransitive • Transitive: Needs an object to receive the action (e.g., She kicked the ball). • Intransitive: Does not need an object (e.g., He laughed).

Subject-Verb Agreement • Singular: The dog barks. (Add -s for he/she/it). • Plural: The dogs bark.

Meaning: Every sentence must have a verb to show what is happening.

Examples: run, eat, is, are

Tenses

Tense shows the time of an action.

Present = Subject + Verb (add -s for he/she/it) Past = Subject + Verb-ed (for regular verbs) Future = Subject + Will + Verb

Meaning: Tense tells whether the action is happening now, before, or later.

Keywords: present : Always, usually, every day, often. past : Yesterday, last week, ago, in 2023. future : Tomorrow, next year, soon, later.

Examples:

  • I eat (present)
  • I ate (past)
  • I will eat (future)

Adjectives

Adjectives describe or modify nouns and pronouns. They provide details about quality, size, age, shape, color, and more.

Formula: Adjective + Noun

Common Placements• Before the noun: The red apple. • After a linking verb: The sky is blue.

Standard Adjective Order When using multiple adjectives, follow this order: 1. Opinion (Beautiful) 2. Size (Big) 3. Age (Old) 4. Shape (Round) 5. Color (Green) 6. Origin (French) 7. Material (Silver) 8. Purpose (Racing)

Meaning: It adds more information about a noun (size, color, quality).

Example: big house, red car

Adverbs

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. They tell us how, when, where, and to what extent something happens..

Formula: Verb + Adverb

The Five Main Types of Adverb • Manner (How): Quickly, loudly, well. • Time (When): Now, yesterday, soon. • Place (Where): Here, outside, everywhere. • Degree (How much): Very, extremely, almost. • Frequency (How often): Always, never, often.

Formation • Most are formed by adding -ly to an adjective (e.g., Quick -> Quickly). • Irregular Adverbs: Some don't follow the -ly rule (e.g., Fast, Hard, Well).

Placement Adverbs can often move around in a sentence: • Beginning: Fortunately, it didn't rain. • Middle: She carefully painted the wall. • End: He speaks softly.

Adverb vs. Adjective • Adjective: Describes a noun (A fast car). • Adverb: Describes an action (He drives fast).

Meaning: It tells how, when, or where something happens.

Example: run quickly

Pronouns

Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition. They change based on their role in a sentence.

Formula: Noun → Pronoun

Personal Pronouns• Subject (The Doer): I, You, He, She, It, We, They. (e.g., She is reading.) • Object (The Receiver): Me, You, Him, Her, It, Us, Them. (e.g., Call him later.) • Possessive (Ownership): Mine, Yours, His, Hers, Ours, Theirs. (e.g., That pen is mine.)

Demonstrative PronounsUsed to point to specific objects: • Near: This (singular), These (plural) • Far: That (singular), Those (plural)

Reflexive PronounsUsed when the subject and object are the same: • Myself, Yourself, Himself, Herself, Itself, Ourselves, Themselves. (e.g., I did it myself.)

Relative PronounsUsed to connect clauses: • Who/Whom (People), Which (Things), That (Both).

Quick Tip: I vs. Me Always test the sentence by removing the other person: • Incorrect: "Jim and me went home." (You wouldn't say "Me went home.") • Correct: "Jim and I went home." (I went home.)

Meaning: It avoids repeating the same noun again and again.

Example: Ali is kind → He is kind

Prepositions

A preposition shows position or direction.

Formula: Preposition + Noun

Meaning: Prepositions show the relationship between a noun (or pronoun) and other parts of the sentence. They typically indicate direction, time, location, and spatial relationships.

Prepositions of Place • At: A specific point (e.g., at the entrance). • In: An enclosed space (e.g., in the room, in the car). • On: A surface (e.g., on the wall, on the table).

Prepositions of Movement :• To: Movement toward a destination (e.g., go to school). • Into: Entering a room or container (e.g., walk into the building). • Across: Movement from one side to the other (e.g., swim across the river).

Common Dependent Prepositions Some verbs/adjectives are always followed by specific prepositions: • Interested in • Afraid of • Good at • Depend on

Example: on the table, under the chair
